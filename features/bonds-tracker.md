# Bonds Tracker

The **Bonds Tracker** panel provides yield and risk data for government and corporate bonds — relevant for Polymarket's fixed income and interest rate markets.

## What It Shows

### Bond Yield Data
Current yield information across major bond categories:
- **US Treasuries** — 2Y, 5Y, 10Y, 30Y yields
- **International sovereign bonds** — UK Gilts, German Bunds, Japanese JGBs, and more
- **Corporate bonds** — investment grade and high yield indexes
- **TIPS** (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities)

### Filtering Options
Filter the bond display by:

| Filter | Options |
|---|---|
| APY (Annual Percentage Yield) | Set minimum / maximum yield threshold |
| Risk Rating | Investment Grade (AAA–BBB), High Yield (BB and below), Unrated |
| Duration | Short (< 2Y), Medium (2–10Y), Long (10Y+) |
| Country / Issuer | Filter by issuing country or company |

### Yield Curve
Visual display of the current yield curve:
- Normal (short-term yields < long-term yields)
- Inverted (short-term yields > long-term yields — historically precedes recession)
- Flat (short and long-term yields are similar)

The shape of the yield curve is one of the most watched macro indicators.

### Credit Spreads
The difference between corporate bond yields and comparable Treasury yields:
- Investment grade spread
- High yield spread

Widening spreads indicate growing credit stress; narrowing spreads signal improving financial conditions.

## Key Concepts

### Yield vs. Price (Inverse Relationship)
Bond prices and yields move in opposite directions. When yields rise, existing bond prices fall — and vice versa. This is important when interpreting bond market movements.

### The 10Y-2Y Spread
The difference between the 10-year and 2-year Treasury yields is one of the most-watched economic indicators:
- **Positive spread** — normal economic environment
- **Negative spread (inverted)** — often precedes economic recession by 12–24 months

### Real Yields
Nominal yield minus inflation expectations = real yield. Rising real yields are generally negative for gold and crypto (higher opportunity cost of holding non-yielding assets).

## How to Use It

**For Fed rate markets:**
1. Check where short-term Treasury yields are — they reflect market expectations for Fed policy
2. Compare to Fed Funds Rate — the spread indicates how much cutting/hiking is priced in

**For recession markets** (e.g., "Will the US enter a recession by [date]?"):
1. Check the 10Y-2Y yield curve — is it inverted?
2. Look at credit spreads — are they widening (stress) or stable?

**For inflation markets:**
1. Check TIPS yields (real yields) vs. nominal Treasury yields
2. The difference = breakeven inflation rate (what the bond market expects for inflation)
